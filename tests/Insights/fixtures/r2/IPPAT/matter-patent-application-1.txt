FIELD OF THE INVENTION

The present invention relates generally to thermal management systems for
high-density computational hardware, and more particularly to closed-loop
fluid-dynamic feedback control for cooling stacked accelerator arrays operating
at thermal design power (TDP) levels exceeding 800 watts per chip.

BACKGROUND

Conventional thermal management approaches for compute accelerators rely on
static coolant flow rates determined at system design time. As workloads vary
in arithmetic intensity, instantaneous thermal output of individual chips can
exceed the nominal design assumptions by factors of 2x or more during burst
phases, leading to thermal throttling, accelerated component aging, or
catastrophic failure. There exists a need for a thermal management system
capable of adapting coolant flow on millisecond timescales based on
chip-level temperature feedback.

SUMMARY OF THE INVENTION

The present invention provides a method and system for adaptive thermal
management in a high-density compute fabric, comprising: (a) a network of
micro-channel coolant passages thermally coupled to a plurality of accelerator
chips arranged in a vertically stacked configuration; (b) per-chip temperature
sensors providing time-series telemetry at a sampling rate of at least 1 kHz;
and (c) a fluid-dynamic feedback controller that adjusts coolant pressure and
flow rate via piezoelectric microvalves in response to predicted thermal
trajectories derived from a state-space model of chip thermal dynamics.

CLAIMS

What is claimed is:

1. A method for thermal management of a high-density compute fabric, comprising:
      (a) receiving, by a feedback controller, time-series temperature signals
          from a plurality of temperature sensors thermally coupled to
          respective accelerator chips of the compute fabric;
      (b) computing, by the feedback controller, a predicted thermal
          trajectory for each accelerator chip based on a state-space model
          parameterized by the time-series temperature signals;
      (c) determining, by the feedback controller, an adjusted coolant flow
          rate for each of a plurality of micro-channel coolant passages
          based on the predicted thermal trajectories; and
      (d) actuating one or more piezoelectric microvalves to effect the
          adjusted coolant flow rate within a control horizon of less than
          ten milliseconds.

2. The method of claim 1, wherein the state-space model is a Kalman filter
   tuned to chip-package thermal time constants of between fifty and two
   hundred milliseconds.

3. The method of claim 1, wherein the time-series temperature signals are
   sampled at a rate of at least 1000 samples per second per chip.

4. The method of claim 1, wherein the high-density compute fabric comprises
   at least one stack of eight accelerator chips, and wherein each accelerator
   chip operates at a thermal design power exceeding 800 watts during nominal
   operation.

5. A system for thermal management of a high-density compute fabric,
   comprising: a plurality of accelerator chips arranged in a vertically
   stacked configuration; a network of micro-channel coolant passages
   thermally coupled to said accelerator chips; a plurality of piezoelectric
   microvalves operable to regulate coolant flow through respective
   micro-channel coolant passages; a plurality of temperature sensors thermally
   coupled to respective accelerator chips; and a feedback controller
   programmed to perform the method of any one of claims 1 to 4.

ABSTRACT

A method and system for adaptive thermal management in a high-density compute
fabric. Time-series temperature signals from sensors thermally coupled to
stacked accelerator chips are processed by a feedback controller that computes
predicted thermal trajectories via a Kalman-filter-based state-space model.
Piezoelectric microvalves regulate coolant flow through micro-channel passages
within a control horizon of less than ten milliseconds, enabling sustained
operation of accelerator chips at thermal design power levels exceeding 800
watts per chip without thermal throttling.
