NON-COMPETE CLAUSE ENFORCEABILITY

Confidentiality: Attorney-Client Privileged

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memorandum analyzes the enforceability of the non-compete clause in the
employment agreement between Adventure Works and the departing employee. We
examine the applicable law in three relevant jurisdictions: Washington State,
California, and New York.

KEY FINDINGS

1. WASHINGTON STATE
   - Non-compete agreements enforceable under RCW 49.62 (effective 2020)
   - Employee must earn > $116,594/year (2026 threshold, adjusted annually)
   - Maximum duration: 18 months
   - Employer must provide independent consideration if signed after start date
   - Garden leave required during non-compete period
   - ANALYSIS: The clause is likely enforceable given employee's salary of
     $185,000 and the 12-month restriction period. However, the geographic
     scope ("worldwide") may be narrowed by a court to the Pacific Northwest.

2. CALIFORNIA
   - Business and Professions Code Section 16600: Non-competes generally void
   - Narrow exceptions: sale of business goodwill, dissolution of partnership
   - AB 1076 (2024) codifies broad prohibition
   - ANALYSIS: The non-compete clause is unenforceable in California.
     If the employee relocates to California, the clause cannot be enforced
     regardless of choice-of-law provisions.

3. NEW YORK
   - Common law framework: enforceability based on reasonableness test
   - Factors: duration, geographic scope, hardship to employee, public interest
   - Recent trend toward stricter scrutiny (pending legislation)
   - Blue pencil doctrine: courts may modify overbroad restrictions
   - ANALYSIS: The 12-month duration is reasonable. The non-solicitation
     component is likely enforceable. The non-competition component depends
     on whether the employee had access to trade secrets.

RECOMMENDATIONS

1. If proceeding to arbitration: Focus on Washington State law as most favorable.
2. Consider settlement in the range of 6-9 months of restricted activity with
   continued salary (garden leave).
3. If employee has relocated to California: Re-evaluate enforcement strategy.
4. Preserve all evidence of trade secret access for potential litigation.
